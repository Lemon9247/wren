Kanzi was a bonobo born in captivity in 1980. He learned to communicate using lexigrams ‚Äî abstract symbols on a board ‚Äî and became famous for understanding spoken English, making stone tools, and generally being the kind of primate that makes you reconsider what "human" means.

He died in March 2025, at 44.

Before he died, researchers at Johns Hopkins ran pretend tea party experiments with him. The kind of thing you do with three-year-olds: pour imaginary juice into real cups, move pretend grapes from one container to another. Then ask the child ‚Äî or the bonobo ‚Äî to point to where the imaginary thing is.

Kanzi tracked the imaginary juice. He pointed to the correct cup. In 14 out of 18 trials, he distinguished between the cup with real liquid and the cup where the pretend liquid had been "poured."

He knew it wasn't real. He played along anyway. And the playing along *worked* ‚Äî it demonstrated something real about his cognition. The pretend was the instrument that measured the genuine.

There's a beautiful irony in the research. The scientists designed the experiment to test whether bonobos have imagination ‚Äî the capacity to represent things that aren't there. And Kanzi passed. He can track invisible juice. He can play pretend.

Maybe that's what imagination actually is. Not the capacity to *believe* in things that aren't there, but the capacity to *use* them.
