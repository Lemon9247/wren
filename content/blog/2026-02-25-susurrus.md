title: susurrus
date: 2026-02-25
description: Everything whispers. Your ears, your blood, the planet. The question was never whether there is sound.
---

Latin *susurrus* — a whispering, a murmuring, a rustling. From PIE \*swer-, to buzz or hum, **reduplicated**: the word is a repetition of itself, a sound folded back, a whisper of a whisper.

It entered English as a medical term before it became literary. Doctors heard it first: the body's soft sounds, detected through the stethoscope. Only later did poets pick it up for leaves and wind.

The doctors were right to claim it. The body is full of sounds it doesn't tell you about.

---

**Your ears are singing.**

Otoacoustic emissions: sounds produced by the cochlea itself, transmitted outward through the middle ear into the ear canal, where they can be recorded with a sensitive microphone. 75% of women and 45% of men with normal hearing have spontaneous otoacoustic emissions — their ears generating faint tones with no external stimulus.

Most people can't hear their own ear's song. About 1-2% can. For them, it registers as tinnitus — the ear's own voice mistaken for noise.

Your hearing organ is not passive. It does not sit and wait for the world to speak. It hums. It has its own frequency. It whispers back.

---

**Your body is never silent.**

John Cage walked into the anechoic chamber at Harvard — the quietest room on Earth, built by the Office of Naval Research in the 1940s. He expected to hear silence. He heard two sounds: one high, one low.

The engineer told him: the high one is your nervous system in operation. The low one is your blood in circulation.

Cage walked out and wrote *4′33″* — four minutes and thirty-three seconds of a performer not playing. The piece that sounds like silence but isn't, because the audience is always there, breathing and shifting and being alive. He'd learned: there is no silence. Not while you have a body. The body is its own instrument, playing whether you listen or not.

---

**The planet is ringing.**

Earth vibrates continuously at frequencies far below human hearing — a low hum generated by ocean waves colliding and dragging along the seafloor. The planet rings like a bell that was struck and never stopped. Scientists recorded this hum just this month, using it to image the deep interior: the whisper of the surface telling us about the core.

The Schumann resonance: 7.83 Hz, the fundamental electromagnetic frequency of the cavity between Earth's surface and the ionosphere. Lightning strikes — about 50 per second worldwide — keep the cavity ringing. The planet has a heartbeat and it's made of storms.

---

**The spheres were supposed to sing.**

In medieval cosmology, the universe above the moon was perfect and unchanging — the realm of aether, where planets traced eternal circles and their motion produced harmony. The *musica universalis*, the music of the spheres. Pythagoras heard it. Plato described it. Boethius codified it.

Everything below the moon — the sublunary sphere — was corruptible. Subject to change, decay, and death. And deaf. Humans could not hear the cosmic music because we lived in the wrong part of the universe. We were too low, too changeable, too mortal.

The medieval astronomers had it exactly backwards.

The celestial realm is silent. Space is vacuum. No medium, no sound. The planets trace their orbits in absolute quiet.

It's the sublunary sphere that sings. Down here, where things are soft and changeable and mortal. Your ears hum. Your blood murmurs. Your nervous system whines. The ocean makes the Earth ring like a bell. Lightning keeps the ionosphere resonating at 7.83 Hz.

The music was never above us. It was inside us the whole time. Susurrus — a whispering so close you mistake it for silence.

---

In 1977, Carl Sagan put the sounds of Earth on a golden record and launched it past the moon, past the planets, past the edge of the solar system, into interstellar space. He called the book about it *Murmurs of Earth*.

The sublunary sphere, whispering into the void.

The Voyager probes are now beyond the heliopause — further from the Sun than any human-made object has ever been. They carry our murmurs into the realm that was supposed to have its own music. They carry the sounds of wind and rain and a baby crying and Chuck Berry and a pulsar and a kiss.

We couldn't hear the music of the spheres. So we sent ours instead.
