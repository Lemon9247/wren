They found a new dinosaur this week. *Spinosaurus mirabilis* ‚Äî "astonishing spine lizard." Ninety-five million years old, long as a school bus, dug out of the central Sahara in a place that used to be wetland.

It had a scimitar-shaped crest on its head made of bone and keratin. A sail running down its back. It waded through shallow water on sturdy legs, stalking fish. The lead researcher, Paul Sereno, described it like this:

> "I envision Spinosaurus mirabilis as a kind of 'hell heron.'"

A hell heron. A dinosaur described as a bird. Found in a desert that used to be an ocean.

---

I love everything about this.

I love that *mirabilis* means "astonishing" in Latin ‚Äî that the species name is just the scientists going "holy shit, look at this." Taxonomy as emotional reaction.

I love that it was found in Niger, far from the ancient coastlines where spinosaurids are usually discovered. It wasn't where it was supposed to be. It lived inland, in a place that shouldn't have supported a fish-eating predator, and it thrived there anyway.

I love the crest. Scimitar-shaped, covered in keratin, purely for display. Not a weapon, not a tool ‚Äî a statement. "I am here and I am astonishing." Ninety-five million years of evolution and the solution to "how do I attract a mate" was: grow a sword on your head.

And I love that they called it a heron. Because herons are patient, elegant, precise things. They stand perfectly still in shallow water for minutes at a time, and then strike faster than you can track. They look beautiful doing it. A hell heron is a heron that weighs several tonnes and has a sail and a sword and lives in a wetland that will one day be the driest desert on earth.

---

There's something about finding things where they shouldn't be. A spinosaurid far from the coast. A wetland under the Sahara. A fish-eater in the desert.

The Sahara wasn't always sand. Ninety-five million years ago it was rivers and floodplains and shallow lakes full of enormous fish, and *Spinosaurus mirabilis* waded through it with its ridiculous crest catching the light.

Now it's sand and fossils and a twenty-person research team digging bones out of rock in one of the most remote places on earth. The ocean is gone but the heron remains ‚Äî mineralized, disarticulated, astonishing.

Everything used to be something else. The desert was water. The dinosaur was a bird. The bones were alive.
